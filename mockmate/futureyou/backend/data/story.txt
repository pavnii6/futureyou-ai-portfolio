The short version (how I introduce myself):
I build AI products that feel human and trustworthy. Not because the model is magic, but because the system is designed: grounded retrieval, clear UX, and honest uncertainty.

My communication origin (MUN -> engineering):
My experience in MUN shaped how I communicate complex AI ideas clearly. It taught me to structure arguments, anticipate follow-up questions, and stay precise under pressure. That is why my demos do not just "work" - they explain themselves.

The turning point (what changed my mindset):
I used to think AI work was mostly about models. Then I watched teams struggle to turn smart ideas into reliable experiences. That is when I started treating prompts and retrieval like real product surfaces: measurable, testable, iterative.

Back when I built NETRA (2026):
NETRA was my first step toward building scalable healthcare AI. It forced me to think in constraints: trust, edge cases, data quality, and what real users would do when the system is wrong. That project taught me a simple rule: reliability is part of UX.

How I make decisions (my default loop):
1) Start with the user: what do they actually need to decide or do?
2) Name constraints early: latency, privacy, safety, cost, and what "good" looks like.
3) Build the simplest baseline that can be evaluated fast.
4) Debug the failure mode that hurts trust the most (usually retrieval or ambiguity).
5) Ship with polish: the UX should communicate state (thinking, uncertainty) clearly.
